Part VIII ? Laurent Series, Annuli of Convergence, and Contour Integrals

We study the Laurent series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{z^n} + \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{5^n}\), determine its largest annulus of convergence, then compute the contour integral of the squared sum on \(|z|=2\). Designed for intuition, rigor, and transfer.

Problem

(i) Consider the Laurent series \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{z^n} + \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{5^n}. \] Determine the largest annulus \( D = \{ z\in\mathbb{C} : r < |z| < R\} \) in which this series converges.

(ii) If \(f: D\to\mathbb{C}\) is the sum of this series on the annulus from (i), compute \[ \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz. \]

Definitions and First Principles

  • Laurent series: A representation of a function as \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z-z_0)^n \), convergent on an annulus centered at \(z_0\).
  • Geometric series: \( \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n = \frac{1}{1-r} \) for \(|r|<1\).
  • Annulus of convergence: intersection of regions where each component series converges.

Quick Intuition

Our series splits into a ?negative powers? part and a ?positive powers? part: \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} z^{-n} \quad\text{and}\quad \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{z}{5}\right)^n. \] The first requires \(|z|>1\); the second requires \(|z|<5\). The function lives where both are true: an annulus \(1<|z|<5\).

(i) Largest Annulus of Convergence

We analyze each sum as a geometric series.

  • Negative powers: \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} z^{-n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (1/z)^n \) converges iff \(|1/z|<1\), i.e. \(|z|>1\).
  • Positive powers: \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (z/5)^n \) converges iff \(|z/5|<1\), i.e. \(|z|<5\).

Therefore the largest annulus is \[ \boxed{\; D = \{z\in\mathbb{C} : 1<|z|<5\} \;} \] and this is maximal since each bound is sharp for geometric series.

Within \(D\), the sums evaluate to \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} z^{-n} = \frac{\frac{1}{z}}{1-\frac{1}{z}} = \frac{1}{z-1}, \qquad \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{z}{5}\right)^n = \frac{1}{1-\frac{z}{5}} = \frac{5}{5-z}. \] So \[ f(z) = \frac{1}{z-1} + \frac{5}{5-z} = \frac{1}{z-1} - \frac{5}{z-5}. \]

Visual: The Annulus

Annulus of convergence: 1 < |z| < 5. The integration contour is the circle |z|=2.

(ii) Compute \(\displaystyle \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz\)

We have \( f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z-1} - \dfrac{5}{z-5} \). Poles are at \(z=1\) and \(z=5\). On \(|z|=2\), only \(z=1\) lies inside.

Method A ? Residue via principal part expansion (fastest)

Write \( f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z-1} + a(z) \) with \( a(z) = -\dfrac{5}{z-5} \) analytic near 1 and \( a(1)=\dfrac{5}{4} \). Then \[ f(z)^2 = \frac{1}{(z-1)^2} + \frac{2a(1)}{z-1} + \cdots \] so \( \operatorname{Res}_{z=1} f(z)^2 = 2a(1) = \dfrac{5}{2} \). Therefore \[ \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz = 2\pi i\,\operatorname{Res}_{z=1} f(z)^2 = 2\pi i\cdot\frac{5}{2} = \boxed{5\pi i}. \]

Method B ? Expand and read residue directly

Expand \[ (f(z))^2 = \left(\frac{1}{z-1} - \frac{5}{z-5}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{(z-1)^2} - \frac{10}{(z-1)(z-5)} + \frac{25}{(z-5)^2}. \] Only the middle term has a simple pole at \(z=1\): its residue is \( -\dfrac{10}{1-5} = \dfrac{5}{2} \). Thus the integral is again \( 2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{5}{2} = \boxed{5\pi i} \).

Method C ? Second-order pole formula

Since \(z=1\) is a double pole of \(f^2\), use \[ \operatorname{Res}_{z=1} f(z)^2 = \lim_{z\to 1} \frac{d}{dz}\Big((z-1)^2 f(z)^2\Big), \] which differentiates a rational function and yields \(\dfrac{5}{2}\). Same conclusion: \(\boxed{5\pi i}\).

All methods agree; Method A is most efficient in practice.

Why This Is Correct

  • Convergence bounds are sharp for geometric series; equality \(|z|=1\) or \(|z|=5\) fails convergence, so the annulus cannot be extended.
  • Residues determine contour integrals of meromorphic functions: only enclosed poles contribute.
  • Double-pole structure at \(z=1\) explains why the integral reduces to the coefficient of \(1/(z-1)\) in the local expansion of \(f^2\); the \(1/(z-1)^2\) term integrates to 0 by Cauchy?s formula with constant numerator.
Final results:
\[ \boxed{\; D = \{ z\in\mathbb{C} : 1<|z|<5 \} \;} \quad\text{and}\quad \boxed{\; \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz = 5\pi i \;}. \]

\section*{Part VIII ? Laurent Series and Contour Integrals}
\begin{card}{Problem}
(i) Consider the Laurent series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{z^n} + \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^n}{5^n}$. Determine the largest annulus $D=\{z\in\mathbb{C}: r<|z|1$. For $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (z/5)^n$, convergence requires $|z/5|<1$, i.e. $|z|<5$. Hence the largest annulus is
\[ D = \{ z\in\mathbb{C} : 1<|z|<5 \}. \]
On $D$,
\[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} z^{-n} = \frac{1}{z-1}, \qquad \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (z/5)^n = \frac{1}{1-z/5} = \frac{5}{5-z}. \]
Therefore $\displaystyle f(z) = \frac{1}{z-1} + \frac{5}{5-z} = \frac{1}{z-1} - \frac{5}{z-5}$.
\end{card}
\begin{card}{(ii) Compute $\int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz$}
Poles of $f$ are at $z=1,5$. On $|z|=2$, only $z=1$ lies inside.
\subsubsection*{Method A (principal part)}
Write $f(z)=\dfrac{1}{z-1}+a(z)$ with $a(z)=-\dfrac{5}{z-5}$ analytic near $1$ and $a(1)=\dfrac{5}{4}$. Then
\[ f(z)^2 = \frac{1}{(z-1)^2} + \frac{2a(1)}{z-1} + \cdots, \quad\text{so}\quad \operatorname{Res}_{z=1} f^2 = 2a(1)=\frac{5}{2}. \]
Hence $\displaystyle \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz = 2\pi i\cdot\frac{5}{2} = 5\pi i$.
\subsubsection*{Method B (algebraic expansion)}
\[ (f(z))^2 = \left(\frac{1}{z-1} - \frac{5}{z-5}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{(z-1)^2} - \frac{10}{(z-1)(z-5)} + \frac{25}{(z-5)^2}. \]
Only the middle term contributes a simple pole at $z=1$ with residue $-\dfrac{10}{1-5}=\dfrac{5}{2}$. The integral is $2\pi i\cdot \dfrac{5}{2}=5\pi i$.
\subsubsection*{Method C (order-2 pole formula)}
For a double pole, $\operatorname{Res}_{z=1} f^2 = \displaystyle \lim_{z\to 1} \frac{d}{dz}((z-1)^2 f(z)^2)$, yielding $\dfrac{5}{2}$, hence $5\pi i$.
\medskip\noindent\textbf{Answer.} $\displaystyle D=\{1<|z|<5\}$ and $\displaystyle \int_{|z|=2} (f(z))^2\,dz = 5\pi i$.
\end{card}
